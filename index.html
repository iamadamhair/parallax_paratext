Mary Leapor greatly admired the work of Alexander Pope, and this poem is influenced by his 1743 poem, "Of the Characters of Women." We can compare the opening of Leapor's poem to that of Pope:

1 Nothing so true as what you once let fall,
2 "Most Women have no Characters at all."
3 Matter too soft a lasting mark to bear,
4 And best distinguish'd by black, brown, or fair.

Here are some lines from the end of Pope's poem:

269 And yet, believe me, good as well as ill,
270 Woman's at best a Contradiction still.

While Leapor did admire Pope, and her poem bears similarities to Pope's poem, Leapor's writing portrays a different attitude from Pope about the contradictions related to women.
In 18th century English law, women were bound by the laws of coverture. Under coverture, the husband and wife were one person, and that one person was the husband in the eyes of the common law. Therefore, as to her personal and property rights, the wife's legal existence was suspended during the marriage and merged into that of the husband. She lost the capacity to contract for herself, or to sue or be sued, without joining the husband as plaintiff or defendant. The husband was entitled to all of the wife's personal property and "choses in action" (lawsuits), and in turn, the husband became liable for all torts committed by the wife, whether before or after marriage.

Wife-beating was both widely tolerated and sanctioned by law in 18th-century England. Husbands were legally entitled to strike their wives in order to "correct" their conduct so long as moderation was the watchword. But even when domestic abuse far exceeded such nice distinctions, wives enjoyed little recourse to the law.
For the upper classes and aristocracy, marriage was often motivated by money rather than love. Because a woman's property came into her husband's control upon marriage, a woman with a substantial dowry or inheritance was a valuable prize.

It is worth noting that Mary Leapor herself was not wealthy; she was the daughter of a gardener and her poetry often reflects her working-class background. Leapor herself worked as a kitchen maid at point in her life.
Prior to the establishment of a divorce court in the middle of the 19th century, England was the only Protestant country in Europe without a specific divorce law. For the vast majority of unhappy couples in England, death-of oneself or one's other half-remained the only means of release from a violent, adulterous, or otherwise unbearable marriage. Alternatives inevitably developed. Desertion, bigamy, or wife-sale proved the last resort for some. Less prosaically, some couples negotiated legal deeds of separation, although these were not always foolproof in a court of law, especially when it came to agreements over women's rights to property or income. More secure were legal separations obtained through the ecclesiastical courts. Known as divorce a mensa et thoro-literally from bed and board-such rulings sanctioned separation but did not permit remarriage. Granted on grounds of extreme cruelty or repeated adultery, they were difficult, expensive, and time-consuming to obtain, especially for women. The vast majority of the plaintiffs were wealthy members of the gentry or aristocracy, and two-thirds were male. Only a tiny handful of powerful, rich, and well-connected couples succeeded in winning a full divorce.
"Artemesia" - Bridget Freemantle, Leapor's friend and patron, is given the name of an ancient patron of the arts. "Artemesia's servant" refers to Leapor, here.

Pamphilia is a character in a sequence of sonnets called Pamphilia to Amphilanthus, by the English Renaissance poet Lady Mary Wroth. "Pamphilia's fate" is that her lover, Amphilanthus, is unfaithful.
